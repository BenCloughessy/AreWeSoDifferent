Are We So Different?

Everyone has a story.

No two are identical.

Each one is like a unique filter over the light of our lives, providing new and fresh color in the world.

Yet how easily do we mistake the filter for the light itself, as we so carelessly sort ourselves by color and shade?

We invite you to experience several stories among many, and to ask yourself:

The Early Days

St. Mary's hospital in Tucson, Arizona is where young Frank came into the world. Although born to caring parents, he quickly came to learn that his place in the family would always be second to his mother's opiate addiction. His father vanished one day when Frank was 4, likely after becoming convinced that he, too, would always be second. Although his mother loved him dearly, Frank often found himself - sometimes for several days - alone and hungry in the apartment. Despite his austere living conditions, he was distraught when CPS removed him from his mother and placed him with a foster family at 8 years of age.

The average child is 7 years old when they enter foster care.¹

"The child welfare system is sometimes described as a highway to homelessness... Nationwide, 50% of the homeless population spent time in foster care."² Entry into this system is often preceded by neglect, drug abuse, and violence.

The Teenage Years

"At age 14 in a small town in Washington State, Natalie's experience with homelessness began. Natalie's dad left her family, and her mom fell into depression and started using methamphetamines. '[I]f she wasn't drunk or high, she was gone,' Natalie noted in one part of Voices of Youth Count's research efforts. For the next six months, Natalie cared for her four younger siblings. She started to miss school and ultimately dropped out. The stress of her circumstance mounted. Through friends, she encountered meth, a drug that had become tragically common in her community. She started using. This only added to conflict with her mom, and, after a fight with her mom's new boyfriend, Natalie was kicked out."⁵
For many, the usual joys and trials of adolescence take a back-seat to the incredible challenges they face.
"Housing instability often has a domino effect and makes it more difficult to complete higher education, maintain stable employment, or manage the stress, trauma, and mental health impacts that go hand-in-hand with homelessness."³

Adulthood

It's almost unheard of for an adult to become homeless without experiencing significant trauma in their lives.
"Homelessness itself is traumatic... and these experiences increase the likelihood of developing trauma-related symptoms and posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD)."³
"I [Jonathan] was teaching at a university, helping students in the engineering department when my relationship broke down just before the first lockdown. I'd been married for 23 years. I left and at the time, because I was still working, I was able to stay in a B&B. But when the outbreak began, that stuffed everything up. My job came to an end and I was put on furlough. I couldn't afford to stay in the hotel anymore so I started sleeping in my car. I ended up living in my car for about six or seven months and that really affected my mental health. I got so low that I tried to take an overdose. My ex called the paramedics and they came, luckily I was okay, but that broke me."⁶

Are we so different?

From the mouth of all those represented
by Frank, by Natalie, and by Jonathan,
the question is asked of us again.
It's our turn to answer.
